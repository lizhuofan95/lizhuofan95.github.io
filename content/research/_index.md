My research projects revolve around similar theoretical and methodological interests at the intersection of knowledge, power, organizations, networks, and computational methods:

1. __Corporate Open Science__
    * Draws on text and network data from *ArXiv*, *GitHub*, and in-depth interviews with clusters of researchers and organizations, my dissertation traces how corporate participation in open science recasts the knowledge infrastructure of computer vision, a rapidly commercializing subfield of artificial intelligence that mobilizes massive human behavioral and biometric data and has potentially transformative consequences on surveillance and stratification. This research has been supported by a SBS Summer Dissertation Fellowship from the College of Social and Behavioral Sciences at the University of Arizona. 

2. __Socio-Semantic Networks__
    * Sociologists have recently turned to network analysis for dynamic models of occupational boundaries. Similarly, in classification systems, category-spanning identities lead to categorical change. How can we use socio-semantic network analysis and word embedding models to model this dynamic and endogenous interaction between identity-making and categorical change?

3. __Networks of Temporalities__
    * (with Corey Abramson and Dan Dohan) How do misalignments between individual experiences of time and the temporal structure of the field stratify actors' ability to activate their endowments of economic, cultural, social, and bodily capital?

4. __Text as data__
    * (with Corey Abramson and Dan Dohan) Coding qualitative data from team-based, multi-site comparative ethnography is tedious and error-prone. How can we use semi-supervised machine learning and pretrained language models to scale human coding in ways that do not fetter but facilitate in-depth interpretation?

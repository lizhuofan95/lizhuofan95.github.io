Research

Today, the science behind advances in many data-driven technologies is increasingly produced by researchers affiliated with corporate laboratories, researchers who nevertheless use and contribute to open-source toolkits, open collaborative platforms, and open access repositories of papers, code, and datasets such as arXiv and GitHub. In the field of computer vision, where a wide range of human behavioral and biometric data are at stake, more than 40% of all papers published at top conferences include authors with corporate affiliations, and more than 1/3 were implemented under open-source frameworks created and maintained at Google and Facebook. This rise of corporate participation in not only the production and commercialization but also the more “open” circulation of scientific knowledge and scientific labor calls for attention to the new organizational forms of commercialized open science.

Draws on text and network data from ArXiv, GitHub, and in-depth interviews with a special focus on Beijing and Silicon Valley as two distinct clusters of researchers and organizations, my dissertation examines how corporate participation in open science shapes the transnational, inter-organizational techno-social-semantic network and temporalities of reseasrchers in computer vision, a rapidly commercializing subfield of artificial intelligence that mobilizes massive human behavioral and biometric data and has potentially transformative consequences on surveillance and stratification in the near future. This research has been supported by a SBS Summer Dissertation Fellowship from the College of Social and Behavioral Sciences at the University of Arizona.

My other projects also revolve around similar theoretical and methodological interests at the intersection of knowledge, power, organizations, networks, and computational methods:

  1. Institutions as techno-social-semantic networks

    • Sociologists have recently turned to flow networks for dynamic models of occupational boundaries. Similarly, in classification systems, category-spanning identities lead to categorical change. How can we use socio-semantic network analysis and word embedding models to model this dynamic and endogenous interaction between identity-making and categorical change?

  2. Fields as networks of temporalities

    • (with Corey Abramson and Dan Dohan) Actors activate their endowments of economic, cultural, social and bodily capital in pursuit of field-specific outcomes, but how does misalignment between individual experiences of time and the temporal structure of the field stratify their ability to act upon capital that they already possess?

  3. Text as data

    • Semantic networks are often constructed in ways that are highly specific to one single, predetermined level of analysis (bigrams, subject–verb–object, sentences, or paragraphs) while losing more nuanced and more distant linguistic features of the text. How can we use contextualized word embedding models to preserve semantic relations at different levels and make semantic network analysis more generalizable and replicable?
    • (with Corey Abramson and Dan Dohan) Coding qualitatiev data from team-based, multi-site comparative ethnography is tedious and error-prone. How can we use semi-supervised machine learning and pretrained language models to scale human coding in ways that do not fetter but facilitate in-depth interpretation?
    • (with Ron Breiger) How can we decompose structural topic models to learn more about cases/documents?
